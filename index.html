From Ramp to Reach:
The Conversion-Proof Phase

One question runs through this room: is the LinkedIn investment building something Microsoft should keep funding? On a fixed Microsoft-funded webinar mandate, FSi entered a new segment and stood up a measurable acquisition engine. It reaches the right buyers, it has built activatable warm audiences, and the assessment engine Microsoft funds against is now live. This room is built to arm FSi for the renewal conversation: what the dollars built, what is converting, and why the next ninety days compounds it.

The room in three minutes
1

The funded motion works

93% of the audience are decision-makers, and the core ICP, nonprofit and healthcare, engages hardest and produces nearly all the conversions. The targeting is proven; this was never an audience problem.

2

The assessment engine is live and ramping

Microsoft funds against assessments. The mid-funnel assessment nudge launched on June 3 against roughly 1,400 nonprofit webinar registrants: one assessment so far, three weeks in, with frequency still building. The top of the funnel built the registrant base the assessment engine now works.

3

The next 90 days is efficiency, not more spend

Weight to the proven nonprofit core, treat SMB as a contained experiment, and activate Meta in July on the accounts LinkedIn cannot reach. Continued Microsoft funding compounds an engine that is already built.

The honest attribution frame

Three tiers, never one number

We do not claim closed revenue we cannot prove. Tier 1 (a click plus a closed deal) needs CRM data FSi does not yet have. What we can show today is the ecosystem of in-market accounts the program surfaced: 213 companies and 298 people on the website, 61 of them decision-makers. That is demand in formation, and it becomes provable pipeline the moment tracking is wired.

The gap worth naming, and fixing. Only 3 of the 213 identified companies had prior ad exposure, so the engine and the website are running on separate tracks today. Retargeting the identified accounts and wiring conversions connects them, one of the fastest lifts available in the account.

The structural risk

One channel carries the whole motion

100% of measured spend is LinkedIn, the right place to start an ABM motion into named accounts. But at ~14x frequency the reachable audience is saturated, and a single-channel program is a concentration risk: any platform, cost, or policy shift hits the entire pipeline at once. The system needs a second and third channel to keep growing and to de-risk.

The reach gap, and the Meta move

LinkedIn delivers to ~15% of the named list; Meta activates the rest

The named target list is roughly 32,000 accounts (about 19K SMB and 13K nonprofit). LinkedIn has delivered at least one impression to 4,713 of them, about 15% penetration. Of those reached, only 604 (13%) get real frequency at 50 or more impressions; 4,109 sit underserved below that, and the remaining roughly 27,000 named accounts get effectively no LinkedIn delivery, the platform cannot match or afford to reach them all. That underserved-and-unreached pool is the Meta activation case: lower CPMs and broader matching extend coverage of the same named list, and Meta engagers route back into LinkedIn retargeting and the warm sequences. We can see the served, underserved, and unreached split directly in the LinkedIn delivery data and hand the export straight to a Meta build. This is agreed as a July test at roughly $1,000 in Meta spend under the current retainer, covering both the nonprofit and SMB accounts LinkedIn cannot reach.
